\chapter{Model development and experimental setup}
\label{chap:setup}

The full process of the geotag recognition task's experimental workflow is presented in this chapter. In Section~\ref{sec:data}, data acquisition, as well as annotation, preprocessing, and partitioning are described. Section~\ref{sec:methods} provides an overview of all four machine learning methods that are being evaluated (the reasons behind each architecture and what the training objective was), and in Section~\ref{sec:baselines} a description of the reference baseline method is provided. Finally, in Section~\ref{sec:metrics}, the evaluation methodology is formalized.

\section{Data collection and preparation}
\label{sec:data}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Data collection: transport complaint records from City Transportation Systems}
\label{sec:data_collection}

The main data collection point is the Operational Complaint Database maintained by the City Public Transport Systems (CTS) - the operator of Astana's public transport system. Each month, it receives around 3\,000 complaints via three input channels; a passenger-facing mobile app, an official city portal and a call centre that transcribes calls into complaints. Every single complaint has to be entered with a mandatory free-text description field (\foreignlanguage{russian}{\textit{описание}}) where the customers have described incidents involving public transport. These include missed bus or tram stops, deviation from planned routes, drivers' behavior and issues related to fare payments as well as conditions of vehicles. The total number of complaints made during this twelve months of operation time was then anonymized by CTS before being handed over to us. All personal identification details had been deleted at that point. There are no geographic coordinates available in relation to each individual complaint.


The raw data contains several elements which distinguish it from other benchmarks used in NLP. Firstly, within approximately 67\,\% of entries there is evidence of intra-sentential switching between Kazakh and Russian. For example \foreignlanguage{russian}{«маршрут 48, уақыты 09:30, остановка Тауелсиздік бағытында жоқ»} (route 48, time 09:30, stop in the direction of Tauelsizidik absent). Secondly, the language in the corpus is informal and unstructured, containing abbreviations, non-standard punctuation and mixed script identifiers. Tao et al.~\cite{tao2022socialNER} demonstrated that these types of noisy characteristics, which are typical of user generated content, can significantly degrade traditional NER systems and therefore necessitate the use of contextual neural models to overcome them. Finally, the distribution of entities is highly uneven; routes and stops make up about 65\,\% of all instances, whereas intersections account for less than 1\,\%, an issue previously documented by Ussenbayeva et al. ~\cite{ussenbayeva2026heterogeneous}, who have also published on this same dataset.


\subsection{Manual BIO annotation in Label Studio}
\label{sec:annotation}

Manual labeling was carried out using \textit{Label Studio}. Each of the three annotators who have native or near-native proficiency in both Russian and Kazakh labeled independently each of the 2750 records according to the six-class entity taxonomy described in section ~\ref{sec:geo_entities} of Chapter~1. During a two week pilot phase, an iteration based on the guidelines for annotation was developed that specified the conventions for spanning boundaries, label priority, and canonicalization for abbreviations and inflected forms. A similar iterative development process has been suggested by Kassab et al. ~\cite{kassab2025russian} for low resource, domain specific annotation.

Each token has been annotated under the BIO tagging system. Inter-anotator agreement (IAA) was used as a method to monitor the quality of the tokens through a protocol where pairwise Cohen's $\kappa$ was calculated at the span level after every batch of 200 records. Overall IAA was found to be $\kappa_{\text{overall}} = 0.84$ (``strong agreement'', $\kappa > 0.80$). Per class IAA ranged from $\kappa = 0.91$ for \texttt{ROUTE\_NUM}to $\kappa = 0.72$ for \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION}. Disputes were resolved by majority vote; disputes involving all three annotators $<2$\,\% of all annotations) were determined by the project supervisor. The final dataset is comprised of 2\,750complaint records exported in JSONL format. See table ~\ref{tab:annotation_stats} for summary of corpus statistics.

\subsection{Preprocessing pipeline}
\label{sec:preprocessing}

Raw complaint data is processed into model readable format via a 5 stage pipeline that is used consistently across each of the four architectures, allowing differences in performance to be attributed to architectural aspects versus processing methods. 

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Unicode normalisation.} Cyrillic Characters: normalized to NFC form. Homophones (i.e. Latin `\texttt{c}' vs. Cyrillic  `\foreignlanguage{russian}{с}'): identified and corrected to use Cyrillic versions. This will prevent sub-word out of vocabulary errors from being encountered by the models when using their respective vocabularies.

  \item \textbf{Whitespace tokenisation.} Records are broken down at each occurrence of white space and/or Unicode Punctuation Characters. Since white space boundaries often coincide with entity span boundaries, punctuation is retained as individual tokens.

  \item \textbf{Subword alignment.} Word-level BIO labels were projected onto sub-word tokens. For a word $w_i$ tokenised into sub-words $s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_m$, the alignment assigns the original label to $s_1$ and a mask value $-100$ to $s_2, \ldots, s_m$:
  \begin{equation}
    \ell'_k =
    \begin{cases}
      \ell_i & \text{if } s_k \text{ is the first sub-word of word } w_i \\
      -100   & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:subword_align}
  \end{equation}
  The value $-100$ is excluded from the loss computation, following the BERT fine-tuning prescription of Devlin et al.~\cite{devlin2019bert}.

  \item \textbf{Sequence truncation.} Sequences were truncated to 512 sub-word tokens for XLM-R and mE5, and to 128 for ruBERT. Corpus analysis confirmed that 98.4\,\% of records fall within 128 whitespace tokens and 99.8\,\% within 512 sub-word tokens, so truncation affects at most 0.2\,\% of records for the primary models.

  \item \textbf{Code-switching handling.} No explicit language identification was performed. Multilingual models (XLM-R, mE5) process mixed-language tokens natively within a shared vocabulary, exploiting the cross-lingual alignment demonstrated by Conneau et al.~\cite{conneau2020xlmr}. The LLM pipeline (Qwen2.5) handled code-switching through bilingual in-context examples in the prompt.
\end{enumerate}

Three properties of Table~\ref{tab:dataset} have direct modelling implications. First, \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION} has only 2 training records and 52 tokens, providing near-zero gradient signal for fine-tuning. Ussenbayeva et al.~\cite{ussenbayeva2026heterogeneous} identify this class as the primary failure mode of encoder-based NER on this corpus, motivating the inclusion of the few-shot LLM approach (Section~\ref{sec:llm}). Second, \texttt{STREET\_NAME} shows a 4.9$\times$ train--test asymmetry (34 vs.\ 167 records), disadvantaging models that rely on lexical memorisation and motivating additional upweighting in the XLM-R objective (Section~\ref{sec:xlmr}). Third, \texttt{BUS\_ID} shows the opposite asymmetry (71 vs.\ 312 records); Iqbal et al.~\cite{iqbal2024multilingual} attribute the robustness of multilingual BERT to distributional shifts partly to its richer sub-word coverage of morphological variants, a property directly relevant here.

\section{Machine learning models}
\label{sec:methods}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Four approaches were implemented. The shared BIO label set contains 13 entity labels ($B$- and $I$-prefix for each of 6 entity types) plus $O$, yielding $K = 13$ output classes. All fine-tuning experiments were conducted on an NVIDIA Tesla T4 GPU (16\,GB VRAM).

\subsection{Model 1: XLM-RoBERTa fine-tuned for token classification}
\label{sec:xlmr}

\subsubsection{Motivation and architecture}

XLM-RoBERTa (\texttt{xlm-roberta-base})~\cite{conneau2020xlmr} is a multilingual transformer encoder pre-trained on 2.5\,TB of filtered CommonCrawl text spanning 100 languages using the masked language modelling objective. Its shared SentencePiece vocabulary of 250\,000 sub-word types covers both Kazakh and Russian, enabling cross-lingual representation alignment beneficial for code-switching input. Conneau et al.~\cite{conneau2020xlmr} demonstrate that XLM-R substantially outperforms mBERT on NER across 16 languages including morphologically rich Slavic and Turkic languages. The base variant contains 12 transformer layers, 768 hidden dimensions, 12 attention heads, and approximately 125 million parameters.

For token classification, the encoder is extended with a linear head. Given the contextual embedding $\mathbf{h}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{768}$ for token $t_i$:
\begin{equation}
  p(y_i = c \mid \mathbf{x}) = \frac{\exp(\mathbf{w}_c^\top \mathbf{h}_i + b_c)}{\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{K} \exp(\mathbf{w}_k^\top \mathbf{h}_i + b_k)}
  \label{eq:xlmr_softmax}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{w}_c \in \mathbb{R}^{768}$ and $b_c \in \mathbb{R}$ are the head parameters for class $c$. The full model is fine-tuned end-to-end.

\subsubsection{Class-weighted training objective}

To prevent the $O$-dominant optimum from suppressing gradients for rare entity classes, a weighted cross-entropy loss is employed:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{XLM-R}} = -\frac{1}{N_{\text{valid}}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{valid}}} w_{y_i} \log p(y_i \mid \mathbf{x}), \qquad w_c = \frac{N_{\text{total}}}{K \cdot N_c}
  \label{eq:class_weight}
\end{equation}
where $N_{\text{valid}}$ is the number of non-masked tokens and $N_c$ is the frequency of label $c$ in the training set. Additionally, \texttt{STREET\_NAME} tokens receive a manual upscaling factor of 1.6 to compensate for the 4.9$\times$ train--test distributional mismatch.

\subsubsection{Learning rate schedule}

Training uses a linear warm-up followed by linear decay~\cite{devlin2019bert}:
\begin{equation}
  \eta(t) =
  \begin{cases}
    \eta_{\max} \cdot \dfrac{t}{T_{\text{warm}}} & t \leq T_{\text{warm}} \\[6pt]
    \eta_{\max} \cdot \dfrac{T - t}{T - T_{\text{warm}}} & t > T_{\text{warm}}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:lr_schedule}
\end{equation}
where $\eta_{\max} = 2 \times 10^{-5}$ and $T_{\text{warm}} = 0.06\,T$.

\subsubsection{Training configuration}

Table~\ref{tab:xlmr_config} summarises the complete hyperparameter configuration. The epoch count of 10 was selected based on validation set performance: macro F1 on rare classes improved up to epoch~8 before plateauing, while frequent-class micro F1 peaked at epoch~5.

\subsection{Model 2: RuBERT (monolingual Russian BERT)}
\label{sec:rubert}

\subsubsection{Motivation}

DeepPavlov RuBERT (\texttt{DeepPavlov/rubert-base-cased})~\cite{kuratov2019rubert} is a BERT-base model~\cite{devlin2019bert} pre-trained on Russian Wikipedia and a Russian news corpus ($\approx$180M parameters). Kassab et al.~\cite{kassab2025russian} establish that RuBERT is the strongest single Russian-language NER model, achieving a 28\,\% precision improvement over commercial alternatives. However, its WordPiece vocabulary was built entirely from Russian text: Kazakh tokens absent from this vocabulary are fragmented into character pieces, producing degraded representations. The expected performance gap between RuBERT and XLM-R on Kazakh-dominant records provides empirical evidence for the value of cross-lingual pre-training — a value confirmed by Iqbal et al.~\cite{iqbal2024multilingual}, who show that cross-lingual transfer rather than model capacity drives the multilingual BERT advantage.

\subsubsection{Subword alignment and prediction}

The alignment rule of Equation~\eqref{eq:subword_align} applies identically via offset mapping:
\begin{equation}
  \ell'_k =
  \begin{cases}
    \ell_i & \text{if } \text{offset}(s_k) = \text{offset}(\text{first sub-word of } w_i) \\
    -100   & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:rubert_align}
\end{equation}
Token labels are assigned by $\hat{y}_i = \arg\max_c\;\operatorname{softmax}(W\mathbf{h}_i + \mathbf{b})_c$. RuBERT is fine-tuned for 3 epochs with batch size 8, learning rate $2 \times 10^{-5}$, maximum sequence length 128, and unweighted cross-entropy loss. The constrained configuration deliberately isolates the effect of pre-training language coverage from confounding factors such as class-weighting and extended training.

\subsection{Model 3: Multilingual E5-base with token classification head}
\label{sec:me5}

\subsubsection{Motivation and architecture}

Multilingual-E5-base (\texttt{intfloat/multilingual-e5-base})~\cite{wang2023e5} is an XLM-RoBERTa-based model ($\approx$278M parameters) trained through a two-stage contrastive procedure on approximately one billion multilingual text pairs. Wang et al.~\cite{wang2023e5} show that this training yields strong multilingual retrieval embeddings. Petasis and Karkaletsis~\cite{petasis2024greek} further demonstrate that E5 embeddings combined with ensemble classifiers achieve F1 = 0.91 on a low-resource Greek NER task, substantially outperforming fine-tuned transformer baselines — motivating mE5's adaptation to the CTS setting. The contrastive training objective is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{E5}} = -\log \frac{\exp\!\left(\cos(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{d}^+) / \tau\right)}{\exp\!\left(\cos(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{d}^+) / \tau\right) + \displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{M} \exp\!\left(\cos(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{d}^-_j) / \tau\right)}
  \label{eq:e5_contrastive}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{q}$ is the query embedding, $\mathbf{d}^+$ is the positive document, $\mathbf{d}^-_j$ are $M$ in-batch negatives, and $\tau$ is a learnable temperature. For token classification, sentence pooling is replaced with a linear projection head:
\begin{equation}
  Z = XW + \mathbf{b}, \quad X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times 768},\; W \in \mathbb{R}^{768 \times K},\; Z \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K}
  \label{eq:me5_head}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Class-weight computation and training configuration}

Class weights are computed over entity tokens only and clipped for stability:
\begin{equation}
  w_c = \operatorname{clip}\!\left(\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{j \in \mathcal{E}} N_j}{\lvert\mathcal{E}\rvert \cdot N_c},\; 0.05,\; 8.0\right)
  \label{eq:me5_weights}
\end{equation}
The upper bound of 8.0 prevents the near-zero \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION} count from producing destabilising weights. mE5 is fine-tuned for 15 epochs, batch size 8, gradient accumulation 2 steps (effective batch 16), learning rate $2 \times 10^{-5}$, AdamW (weight decay 0.01, warm-up ratio 0.1). The longer schedule compensates for the misalignment between the contrastive pre-training objective and the discriminative NER objective.

\subsection{Model 4: Qwen2.5-7B via few-shot prompting}
\label{sec:llm}

\subsubsection{Motivation}

Fine-tuned encoders are constrained by available labelled data: the \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION} class has only 2 training records. Few-shot prompting circumvents this by exploiting knowledge acquired during large-scale pre-training. Keraghel et al.~\cite{keraghel2024survey} report that instruction-tuned LLMs are well-suited to low-count entity types, with GPT-4 achieving F1 = 0.83 on CoNLL-2003 in zero-shot mode. Mai et al.~\cite{mai2024geollm} demonstrate that LLMs improve geoparsing for underrepresented spatial entity types, directly relevant to the rare classes in the CTS corpus.

Qwen2.5~\cite{yang2024qwen} is an open-weight LLM series (0.5B--72B parameters). The 7B instruction-tuned variant supports 29+ languages including Russian and Kazakh and produces reliable structured JSON output through SFT and RLHF training. It was deployed locally via \textit{Ollama}.

\subsubsection{Prompt construction}

The inference prompt is constructed as:
\begin{equation}
  P_i = \text{SYS} \oplus E_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus E_{12} \oplus x_i
  \label{eq:prompt}
\end{equation}
where SYS defines the entity taxonomy and extraction rules in both Russian and Kazakh, and $E_1, \ldots, E_{12}$ are 12 in-context examples (2 per entity type). For \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION}, both available training instances were included. The required JSON output schema is:
\begin{verbatim}
  {"ROUTE_NUM": [...], "BUS_ID": [...], "PLATE_NUM": [...],
   "STOP_NAME": [...], "STREET_NAME": [...],
   "STREET_INTERSECTION": [...]}
\end{verbatim}
This selection strategy follows the recommendation of Manjavacas et al.~\cite{manjavacas2026cvs}, who show that covering all target fields in in-context examples improves structured extraction in LLMs.

\subsubsection{Post-filtering and inference configuration}

LLM extractors frequently produce hallucinated spans~\cite{keraghel2024survey}. A post-filter retains only spans satisfying:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathcal{E}}_i^{(c)} = \bigl\{v \in \tilde{\mathcal{E}}_i^{(c)} \;\big|\; |v| \geq 2 \;\wedge\; v \notin \mathcal{W}_{\text{stop}} \;\wedge\; \phi_c(x_i)\bigr\}
  \label{eq:postfilter}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{W}_{\text{stop}}$ is a curated set of noise tokens and $\phi_c(x_i)$ is a class-specific keyword check (e.g., \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION} requires a spatial conjunction such as \foreignlanguage{russian}{«угол»} or \foreignlanguage{russian}{«пересечение»} in the source text). Due to the computational cost of serial inference, Qwen2.5 is evaluated on the first 300 test records; comparisons with encoder models in Chapter~3 are restricted to this shared subset. \section{Baseline methods}
\label{sec:baselines}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Four baselines were implemented to contextualise main model results, ordered from knowledge-free to representation-based.

\subsection{Regex-based rule extraction}
\label{sec:regex_baseline}

A deterministic extractor using hand-crafted regular expressions was implemented for the three structurally regular identifier types: \texttt{ROUTE\_NUM}, \texttt{BUS\_ID}, and \texttt{PLATE\_NUM}. Patterns were derived from inspection of 100 training records per class. Route numbers were matched by numeric sequences optionally preceded by Kazakh/Russian route keywords or \foreignlanguage{russian}{«№»}; plate numbers by the standard Kazakhstani alphanumeric format. The approach parallels the regex lower bounds of Santos et al.~\cite{santos2021address}: near-perfect precision on conforming formats, zero recall on unseen variants. Because \texttt{STOP\_NAME}, \texttt{STREET\_NAME}, and \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION} lack reliable structural patterns, the extractor assigns \texttt{O} to all their tokens.

\subsection{Frozen XLM-R linear probe}
\label{sec:frozen_baseline}

A linear probe was trained atop frozen XLM-RoBERTa representations~\cite{conneau2020xlmr}, with only the classification head $W \in \mathbb{R}^{768 \times K}$ updated. This baseline isolates the contribution of task-specific fine-tuning from that of the pre-trained representations alone: the performance gap relative to Model~1 quantifies how much representational adaptation the CTS entity types require. A related design is used by Petasis and Karkaletsis~\cite{petasis2024greek}, who find that strong pre-trained embeddings can partially compensate for limited labelled data in low-resource NER.

\subsection{CRF with handcrafted features}
\label{sec:crf_baseline}

A Conditional Random Field~\cite{lafferty2001crf} was trained on the following token-level feature categories:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Surface form:} lowercase word form; character prefix and suffix of lengths 2, 3, and 4.
  \item \textbf{Orthographic:} \textit{is\_digit}, \textit{is\_upper}, \textit{is\_mixed\_script} (Cyrillic + Latin), \textit{has\_hyphen}.
  \item \textbf{Contextual window:} word form and orthographic flags of the two preceding and two following tokens.
  \item \textbf{Gazetteer:} binary membership in the Astana transit stop registry (CTS operational database) and the OpenStreetMap street name database.
\end{itemize}

Training used L-BFGS with L1 + L2 regularisation ($\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = 0.1$), converging within 300 iterations. Santos et al.~\cite{santos2021address} and Tao et al.~\cite{tao2022socialNER} both confirm 10--15 F1 point gaps between BERT-based and CRF approaches on analogous tasks; the CTS CRF baseline tests whether the same gap holds here.

\subsection{Most-frequent-class (MFC) baseline}
\label{sec:mfc_baseline}

The MFC baseline assigns $O$ to every token. Every entity class receives $F_{1,c}^{\text{MFC}} = 0$, so both macro and micro entity F1 are identically zero. This baseline anchors the evaluation floor: any model failing to exceed $F_{1,\text{macro}} = 0$ has learned nothing beyond the majority-class prior. It also demonstrates that token-level accuracy of 85.2\,\% is entirely uninformative for the NER task.

\section{Evaluation metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

All models are evaluated using span-level metrics with a strict exact-match protocol: a predicted span $\hat{s} = (\hat{e}, \hat{l}, \hat{r})$ is a true positive only if:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{e} = e^* \quad \wedge \quad \hat{l} = l^* \quad \wedge \quad \hat{r} = r^*
  \label{eq:tp_condition}
\end{equation}
Any boundary mismatch is counted as both a false positive and a false negative. This strict criterion reflects the operational requirement that extracted spans must exactly match GIS stop or street registry keys.

\subsection{Per-class precision, recall, and F1-score}
\label{sec:per_class_metrics}

For each entity class $c \in \mathcal{E}$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{P}_c = \frac{\mathrm{TP}_c}{\mathrm{TP}_c + \mathrm{FP}_c}, \qquad
  \mathrm{R}_c = \frac{\mathrm{TP}_c}{\mathrm{TP}_c + \mathrm{FN}_c}, \qquad
  F_{1,c} = \frac{2\,\mathrm{P}_c\,\mathrm{R}_c}{\mathrm{P}_c + \mathrm{R}_c}
  \label{eq:per_class_f1}
\end{equation}
Per-class metrics are the primary diagnostic instrument. They reveal the heterogeneous performance pattern documented by Ussenbayeva et al.~\cite{ussenbayeva2026heterogeneous}: a model may achieve F1 $> 0.90$ on \texttt{ROUTE\_NUM} while simultaneously achieving F1 $= 0.00$ on \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION} — a disparity invisible in any aggregate score.

\subsection{Macro- and micro-averaged F1}
\label{sec:agg_metrics}

\textit{Macro-averaged F1} weights all entity types equally:
\begin{equation}
  F_{1,\text{macro}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|} \sum_{c \in \mathcal{E}} F_{1,c}
  \label{eq:macro_f1}
\end{equation}
This is the primary aggregate metric because it penalises models that sacrifice minority-class performance for high throughput on frequent classes~\cite{keraghel2024survey}. A model achieving F1 = 0.95 on the two frequent classes but F1 = 0.00 on the four remaining classes records a macro F1 of only $0.32$, correctly reflecting its limited operational utility.

\textit{Micro-averaged F1} aggregates counts across all classes:
\begin{equation}
  F_{1,\text{micro}} = \frac{2 \displaystyle\sum_{c} \mathrm{TP}_c}{2\displaystyle\sum_{c} \mathrm{TP}_c + \displaystyle\sum_{c} \mathrm{FP}_c + \displaystyle\sum_{c} \mathrm{FN}_c}
  \label{eq:micro_f1}
\end{equation}
It is dominated by frequent classes and reported as a secondary metric for comparability with the broader NER literature.

\subsection{Boundary stability}
\label{sec:boundary_stability}

Boundary stability measures the fraction of correctly typed predicted spans with exact boundaries:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{BS}_c = \frac{\bigl|\bigl\{(\hat{e},\hat{l},\hat{r}) \in \hat{S}_c \;:\; \exists\,(e^*,l^*,r^*) \in S^*_c,\; \hat{l} = l^* \wedge \hat{r} = r^*\bigr\}\bigr|}{|\hat{S}_c|_{\text{type-only}}}
  \label{eq:bs}
\end{equation}
A model with high precision but low boundary stability produces correctly typed but incorrectly delimited spans. Such errors are operationally costly: an extraneous prefix word (e.g., \foreignlanguage{russian}{«остановка»} included within a \texttt{STOP\_NAME} span) causes a GIS lookup failure even when the entity type is correct. Ussenbayeva et al.~\cite{ussenbayeva2026heterogeneous} identify boundary errors as a recurring failure mode for multi-word toponym classes.

\subsection{Confusion matrix analysis}
\label{sec:confusion_matrix}

A token-level confusion matrix across all 13 BIO labels plus $O$ is computed. The matrix entry $C_{ij}$ counts tokens with gold label $i$ predicted as $j$. Two axes are diagnostically important. The \textit{identifier confusion} axis (between \texttt{BUS\_ID} and \texttt{PLATE\_NUM}) measures conflation of types sharing nearly identical surface forms; Ussenbayeva et al.~\cite{ussenbayeva2026heterogeneous} report 91 such misclassifications in baseline experiments. The \textit{toponym confusion} axis (between \texttt{STOP\_NAME} and \texttt{STREET\_NAME}) measures conflation of multi-word spatial entities that frequently co-occur. High values on either axis indicate the model detects entity supertype surface forms without acquiring sensitivity to the contextual cues that determine subtype.

The five metrics are complementary. Macro F1 indicates overall viability; per-class F1 identifies which entity types drive shortfalls; boundary stability determines geocoding readiness; and the confusion matrix localises misclassification pathways for future targeted improvement. Together they provide a multi-dimensional framework aligned with the operational requirements of the CTS deployment scenario described in Chapter~1~\cite{keraghel2024survey}.
