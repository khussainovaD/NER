\section{Experimental setup and training configurations}
\label{sec:exp_setup}

All fine-tuning experiments were conducted on a single NVIDIA Tesla T4 GPU (16\,GB VRAM) using the Hugging Face \texttt{transformers} library (v4.40). The Qwen2.5:7B model was served locally via Ollama on the same machine using CPU inference (32\,GB RAM), which explains the substantially longer inference time relative to GPU-accelerated encoders.

\section{Per-model results and error analysis}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{XLM-RoBERTa: results and error analysis}
\label{sec:xlmr_results}

XLM-RoBERTa demonstrates the strongest performance on high-frequency, structurally regular entities. \texttt{ROUTE\_NUM} achieves near-state-of-the-art F1\,=\,0.920, attributed to the consistent numeric format of route identifiers and their high training frequency. \texttt{BUS\_ID} performance is moderate (F1\,=\,0.735), while \texttt{PLATE\_NUM} and \texttt{STOP\_NAME} achieve comparable scores ($\approx$0.633--0.650). \texttt{STREET\_NAME} achieves only F1\,=\,0.294 due to class imbalance and morphological variation. \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION} receives zero detections (F1\,=\,0.000), reflecting extreme scarcity and structural complexity.

Error analysis reveals that most \texttt{STREET\_NAME} errors involve boundary confusion: the model identifies the correct street-name token but includes or excludes adjacent case-suffix tokens, producing partial-match failures under exact-boundary evaluation. \texttt{PLATE\_NUM} false positives arise from bus ID strings that share the alphanumeric format of licence plates.

\subsection{ruBERT: results and limitations on Russian--Kazakh mixed text}
\label{sec:rubert_results}

ruBERT exhibits severely degraded performance (macro F1\,=\,0.223), consistent with the code-switching nature of the corpus. The model fails entirely on \texttt{BUS\_ID}, \texttt{STREET\_NAME}, and \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION} (F1\,=\,0.000 for all three), and achieves only F1\,=\,0.667 for \texttt{ROUTE\_NUM}. Analysis of the confusion matrix reveals systematic label collapse toward \texttt{STOP\_NAME}, the most frequent class. This behaviour is a known failure mode of monolingual models applied to out-of-vocabulary code-switching text.

\subsection{Multilingual E5-base: results and error analysis}
\label{sec:me5_results}

Me5-base achieves the highest recall (0.653) and competitive F1-score (0.608). \texttt{ROUTE\_NUM} performance is near-identical to XLM-RoBERTa (F1\,=\,0.922). \texttt{BUS\_ID} (F1\,=\,0.692) and \texttt{STOP\_NAME} (F1\,=\,0.610) show improved recall relative to XLM-R. The model fails on \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION} (F1\,=\,0.000), consistent with the encoder baselines.

The most striking finding is the catastrophic failure of ruBERT (macro F1\,=\,0.223) on the code-switching corpus, despite the Russian-dominant character of the text. The 67\,\% rate of intra-sentence Kazakh-Russian mixing is sufficient to severely degrade a monolingual model. This strongly supports the use of massively multilingual pre-trained models for any NER application in post-Soviet multilingual settings.

The superior overall performance of Qwen2.5 despite receiving no gradient updates raises important practical considerations. The LLM advantage stems from: (1)~its 7B parameter scale enabling richer compositional reasoning for complex entities; (2)~instruction tuning on diverse multilingual data; and (3)~entity-level JSON output, which bypasses BIO boundary errors that systematically penalise fine-tuned models.

However, fine-tuned encoders retain advantages for structurally regular entities (\texttt{ROUTE\_NUM} F1\,$\approx$\,0.92) and offer orders-of-magnitude better throughput: processing the full 825-sample test set takes $\approx$12--18 seconds on GPU for encoders, versus 4+ hours for 300 Qwen2.5 samples.

\section{Analysis of challenging cases (rare entities, \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION}, code-switching, noisy input)}
\label{sec:hard_cases}

\textbf{STREET\_INTERSECTION.} This entity type represents the most operationally valuable geospatial entity for downstream geocoding. Its near-universal failure in fine-tuned models (only Qwen2.5 detects it, at recall\,=\,0.500) reflects two compounding problems: (1)~only 74 training examples exist across the entire corpus, making gradient-based learning unreliable; and (2)~the entity spans 5--10 tokens with a variable internal structure that includes two distinct street names joined by a conjunction word.

\textbf{Code-switching entities.} Stop names in the CTS corpus frequently appear in Kazakh (\foreignlanguage{russian}{«аялдама Қобыланды»}), Russian (\foreignlanguage{russian}{«остановка Кобыланды»}), or mixed forms within the same sentence. XLM-R and mE5 handle this robustly; ruBERT collapses Kazakh-language stop names into \texttt{O} or misclassifies them as \texttt{STOP\_NAME} with incorrect boundaries.

\textbf{Noisy input.} Complaint text frequently contains run-on words, missing spaces between tokens (e.g., \foreignlanguage{russian}{«маршрут47»} instead of \foreignlanguage{russian}{«маршрут 47»}), and inconsistent capitalisation. All models show recall degradation on records with high noise density. The Qwen2.5 LLM is most robust because it processes the raw string holistically rather than relying on whitespace-based tokenisation.

\textbf{Label confusion between similar entity types.} \texttt{BUS\_ID} and \texttt{PLATE\_NUM} share similar alphanumeric surface forms, causing all models to exhibit bidirectional confusion between these two classes. Disambiguation requires broader contextual cues (e.g., presence of the word \foreignlanguage{russian}{«госномер»} for plate numbers) that fine-tuned encoders learn imperfectly due to limited training examples.

\section{Selection of the optimal model and its integration into an inference pipeline for CTS complaint processing}
\label{sec:integration}

\textbf{Model selection.} For real-time CTS deployment, XLM-RoBERTa is the recommended model based on the following criteria:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Macro F1 of 0.539 with GPU throughput of $\approx$12 seconds per 825 records ($\approx$4\,000 records/minute on a T4), satisfying the real-time constraint.
  \item Precision of 0.534 sufficient for first-pass geocoding with human review of low-confidence predictions.
  \item Near-perfect \texttt{ROUTE\_NUM} detection (F1\,=\,0.920) and strong \texttt{STOP\_NAME} recall (0.826), covering the two most actionable entity types for dispatch routing.
\end{enumerate}

For batch analytical workflows where latency is not a constraint, Qwen2.5 is preferred due to its higher macro F1 (0.635) and unique ability to detect \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION} entities.

\textbf{Inference pipeline.} The recommended production pipeline for CTS complaint processing operates as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Preprocessing:} Unicode normalisation and whitespace tokenisation.
  \item \textbf{Inference:} XLM-RoBERTa token classification, producing BIO label sequences.
  \item \textbf{Span extraction:} BIO decoding to produce entity spans with labels and confidence scores (maximum softmax probability within the span).
  \item \textbf{Confidence thresholding:} Spans with maximum confidence $< 0.6$ are flagged for human review.
  \item \textbf{Geocoding:} Extracted \texttt{STOP\_NAME}, \texttt{STREET\_NAME}, and \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION} spans are passed to the Astana city geocoder API for coordinate resolution.
  \item \textbf{Output:} Structured JSON record with extracted entities, confidence scores, and resolved coordinates.
\end{enumerate}

Future work should investigate targeted data augmentation for \texttt{STREET\_INTERSECTION} using synthetic intersection descriptions generated from city street graph databases, back-translation between Kazakh and Russian to increase example diversity, and hybrid architectures combining an encoder for structured identifiers with an LLM for complex geographic spans.
