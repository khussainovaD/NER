\chapter*{Introduction}
\phantomsection
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Introduction}

The accelerating digitisation of urban transport infrastructure has generated an ever-growing volume of unstructured citizen feedback. Public transport authorities now receive thousands of complaint messages daily through mobile applications, web portals, and call-centre transcriptions. Each record contains a free-form description in which passengers report operational failures: missed stops, route deviations, driver misconduct, and payment issues. Embedded within this unstructured text are geographic references --- route numbers, stop names, street names, vehicle identifiers, and intersection descriptions --- that, if extracted automatically, can power real-time situational awareness dashboards, smart route-optimisation engines, and automated incident-routing systems. The growing volume of these records, combined with the operational cost of manual triage, highlights a critical need for effective and broadly available automated text analysis capabilities. Meanwhile, recent advances in natural language processing and transformer-based language models have made it possible to detect semantic patterns in noisy multilingual text, rendering automatic geotag extraction an immediate and realistic prospect.

Despite the expanding body of research on named entity recognition, most existing systems have been developed for well-resourced languages and generic domains such as news articles or biomedical literature. These systems lack the specificity needed for transport-domain operational text in low-resource bilingual settings. In the context of Astana, Kazakhstan, where City Transportation Systems (CTS) processes approximately 3\,000 complaint records per month, the target text is written in a mixture of Kazakh and Russian with frequent code-switching, informal register, domain-specific abbreviations, and inconsistent punctuation. No prior work targets the specific combination of Kazakh-Russian code-switching, transport-domain complaint text, a six-class entity taxonomy including structurally complex composite entities, and a comparative evaluation of architecturally distinct machine learning approaches under identical experimental conditions. This gap represents a substantial drawback, as no appropriate tool currently exists to support automated complaint triage for a multilingual Central Asian public transport operator.

The current study aims to address this gap by developing and comparing multiple machine learning approaches for automatic geotag recognition in multilingual textual data, motivated by the operational needs of CTS.

To realise this goal, the study (i) reviews the state of the art in named entity recognition and geotag extraction across rule-based, statistical, neural, and large language model paradigms; (ii) collects, preprocesses, and annotates a domain-specific multilingual dataset of 2\,750 public transport complaint records with gold-standard BIO labels for six entity types; (iii) develops and evaluates four machine learning approaches --- fine-tuned XLM-RoBERTa, fine-tuned DeepPavlov ruBERT, fine-tuned Multilingual-E5-base, and few-shot Qwen2.5 LLM inference --- together with four baseline methods against per-class and macro-averaged precision, recall, and F1 metrics; and (iv) integrates the best-performing architecture into a recommended inference pipeline suitable for real-time CTS complaint processing. The research object is the process of named entity recognition in multilingual public transport complaint texts; the research subject is the family of machine learning models and algorithms for automatic extraction of six domain-specific geotag entity types from noisy, code-switched Kazakh-Russian natural language text.

The dissertation is composed of four chapters. Chapter~1 outlines the theoretical foundations by analysing the linguistic and operational characteristics of geographic named entities in transport-domain text. It reviews current approaches to automatic named entity recognition from rule-based and statistical methods through transformer encoders and large language models, examines the specific challenges of Kazakh-Russian code-switching and morphological complexity, and summarises 21 key related works. On this basis, the chapter formulates the research problem, defines the six entity types, specifies the data requirements for model training, and establishes the quantitative criteria for evaluating model effectiveness.

Chapter~2 addresses model development and experimental setup, presenting the data collection and annotation pipeline, the preprocessing methodology for handling code-switching and subword alignment, the training configurations for each of the four main models, the design of the four baseline methods, and the evaluation metrics used throughout the study.

Chapter~3 presents the comparative experimental results, including per-model performance tables, error analysis of challenging cases --- rare entities, code-switching failures, and noisy input --- and a cross-model comparison leading to a deployment recommendation for the CTS production environment.

Chapter~4 summarises the principal findings, states the research contributions, and outlines directions for future work including targeted data augmentation for rare entities and hybrid architectures combining encoder and LLM components.

The applied value of this work lies in its direct relevance to real-world public transport management. By enabling rapid and accurate geotag extraction from citizen complaints, the proposed system allows transport operators to respond to incidents faster, identify geographic hotspots automatically, and reduce dependence on manual triage operators. In addition, the annotated dataset and comparative results provide a baseline for subsequent research at the intersection of multilingual NLP and smart city infrastructure.
